Malaria

A serious disease spread by mosquitoes that affects millions in Sierra Leone

What Causes Malaria?

Malaria is caused by tiny parasites called Plasmodium that live inside certain mosquitoes. When an infected mosquito bites you, the parasites enter your bloodstream and travel to your liver. Once in your liver, they multiply and spread to your red blood cells, making you sick.

How it spreads:

  • Female Anopheles mosquitoes bite during dawn and dusk
  • The mosquito must be infected with the malaria parasite
  • One mosquito bite can infect a person
  • Malaria cannot spread from person to person through touch or contact

Symptoms of Malaria

Malaria symptoms usually appear 10-15 days after a mosquito bite, but can take up to 30 days. Early symptoms are like flu:

  • High fever - Temperature rises to 40°C or higher
  • Chills and body aches - Severe shaking and muscle pain
  • Headache - Strong headache with fever
  • Fatigue - Extreme tiredness and weakness
  • Vomiting and diarrhea - Stomach upset and nausea
  • Jaundice - Yellowing of eyes and skin (in severe cases)

⚠️ When to seek help: If you have fever, chills, and body aches, go to a health center or hospital immediately for a blood test.

How to Prevent Malaria

Best Prevention Methods:

  • Sleep under a mosquito net every night Even if it's hot, a net protects you. Tuck it under your mattress so mosquitoes cannot enter.
  • Use insecticide-treated bed nets These nets are treated with poison that kills mosquitoes on contact.
  • Wear protective clothing During dawn and dusk, wear long sleeves and long pants to cover your skin.
  • Use mosquito repellent Apply insect repellent with DEET on exposed skin, or use natural alternatives like lemongrass oil.
  • Drain stagnant water Empty water from buckets, tires, and flower pots. Mosquitoes breed in standing water.
  • Keep your home clean Repair torn screens on windows and doors to prevent mosquitoes from entering.
  • Take antimalarial medication If traveling to high-risk areas, ask your doctor about preventive medication.

Treatment and Management

Early Treatment Saves Lives:

If you suspect you have malaria, get tested and treated immediately. The sooner you start treatment, the better your chances of recovery.

  • Get a blood test - A simple finger prick test confirms malaria
  • Take antimalarial drugs - Your doctor will prescribe the right medication
  • Complete the full course - Take all medicines as prescribed, even if you feel better
  • Rest and drink fluids - Stay hydrated and get plenty of rest
  • Take fever-reducing medicine - Paracetamol or ibuprofen can reduce fever and pain

Note: Common malaria treatments in Sierra Leone include artemisinin-based drugs like artemether and arteether. Always use medicines prescribed by a qualified healthcare provider.

Typhoid Fever

A bacterial infection spread through contaminated food and water

What Causes Typhoid?

Typhoid is caused by bacteria called Salmonella typhi. This bacterium lives in the gut of infected people and spreads through contaminated food and water. In areas without clean water and proper sanitation, typhoid spreads easily.

How it spreads:

  • Drinking water contaminated with the typhoid bacteria
  • Eating food prepared by someone who has typhoid and did not wash their hands
  • Eating raw or undercooked seafood from contaminated water
  • Using toilets that are not properly separated from water sources
  • Poor sanitation and hygiene practices

Symptoms of Typhoid

Symptoms usually appear 6-30 days after infection. Typhoid has three stages:

First Week:

  • Fever - Temperature rises gradually to 39-40°C
  • Headache - Severe and constant
  • Body aches - Muscles and joints hurt
  • Weakness - Extreme fatigue and tiredness
  • Loss of appetite - Don't feel hungry

Second and Third Weeks:

  • High fever - May remain at 40-41°C
  • Rose spots rash - Small pink spots on chest
  • Diarrhea or constipation - Depends on the person
  • Vomiting - Stomach upset
  • Abdominal pain - Pain in the belly
  • Confusion - May seem confused or delirious

⚠️ When to seek help: If fever lasts more than 3-4 days with body aches and headache, visit a health center for testing.

How to Prevent Typhoid

Prevention Strategies:

  • Drink safe water Boil water before drinking, or use bottled water. Boil for at least 1 minute to kill bacteria.
  • Wash fruits and vegetables Wash with clean water before eating. Peel when possible.
  • Practice proper hand hygiene Wash hands with soap and water before eating and preparing food, and after using the toilet.
  • Cook food thoroughly Make sure meat, fish, and seafood are fully cooked. Do not eat raw or undercooked food.
  • Use clean toilets Use a proper latrine or toilet, not an open area. Ensure it is far from water sources.
  • Get vaccinated Typhoid vaccine is available and effective. Ask your health worker about vaccination.
  • Store food properly Keep food in clean, covered containers. Protect from insects and animals.

Treatment and Management

Treatment Approach:

Typhoid is treated with antibiotics. Early treatment prevents serious complications.

  • Get tested - Blood test confirms typhoid diagnosis
  • Take antibiotics - Your doctor will prescribe antibiotics like ciprofloxacin or ceftriaxone
  • Complete full course - Take all medicines as prescribed (usually 7-14 days)
  • Stay hydrated - Drink plenty of clean water and fluids
  • Rest - Get plenty of sleep to help your body fight infection
  • Eat nutritious food - When you feel better, eat healthy foods to regain strength

Care During Recovery:

  • Keep the patient isolated to prevent spreading to others
  • Help the patient use the toilet safely
  • Wash your hands after helping them
  • Do not prepare food for others if you have typhoid

Note: Complications like intestinal perforation can be life-threatening. Seek urgent medical help if symptoms get worse.

Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)

A chronic condition that increases risk of heart disease and stroke

What Causes Hypertension?

Hypertension means your blood pressure is consistently too high. Blood pressure is the force of blood pushing against your artery walls. Normal blood pressure is less than 120/80. High blood pressure is 140/90 or higher.

Risk Factors:

  • Age - Risk increases as you get older
  • Family history - If parents or siblings have high blood pressure
  • Too much salt - Eating salty foods raises blood pressure
  • Overweight - Extra weight puts stress on your heart
  • Lack of exercise - Sedentary lifestyle increases risk
  • Stress - Chronic stress can raise blood pressure
  • Alcohol and smoking - Both damage blood vessels
  • Kidney disease - Can cause or worsen high blood pressure

Symptoms of Hypertension

High blood pressure is called a "silent killer" because most people have no symptoms. Many people do not know they have it until they get it checked.

When symptoms do occur:

  • Headaches - Especially at the back of the head
  • Shortness of breath - Difficulty breathing or breathlessness
  • Chest pain - Pain or pressure in the chest
  • Nosebleeds - Spontaneous bleeding from the nose
  • Dizziness - Feeling lightheaded or unbalanced
  • Blurred vision - Trouble seeing clearly

⚠️ Important: Do not wait for symptoms. Get your blood pressure checked regularly, especially if you are over 40 or have risk factors.

How to Prevent and Manage Hypertension

Lifestyle Changes (Most Important):

  • Reduce salt intake Use less salt in cooking and avoid salty snacks. Aim for less than 1 teaspoon (5g) daily.
  • Exercise regularly Walk, dance, or do any physical activity for at least 30 minutes, 5 days a week.
  • Maintain healthy weight If overweight, losing 5-10% of body weight can significantly lower blood pressure.
  • Eat a healthy diet Eat plenty of fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and lean proteins. Avoid fried and processed foods.
  • Reduce stress Practice relaxation techniques like deep breathing, meditation, or yoga.
  • Limit alcohol If you drink, do so in moderation. Men should have no more than 2 drinks per day, women 1 drink.
  • Do not smoke Smoking damages blood vessels and raises blood pressure. Quit smoking today.
  • Get quality sleep Sleep 7-8 hours each night. Poor sleep can increase blood pressure.

Medical Management:

  • Regular monitoring - Check your blood pressure at least once a year, or as recommended by your doctor
  • Take prescribed medication - If lifestyle changes are not enough, your doctor may prescribe blood pressure medication
  • Follow doctor's advice - Take medications as prescribed and attend follow-up appointments
  • Know your numbers - Understand your blood pressure readings (e.g., 140/90)

Foods That Help Lower Blood Pressure:

  • Leafy greens (spinach, cassava leaves)
  • Bananas and other potassium-rich fruits
  • Whole grains
  • Fish and lean meats
  • Low-fat dairy products
  • Nuts and seeds
  • Garlic and onions

Treatment and Complications

Why Treatment is Important:

Untreated high blood pressure can cause serious health problems:

  • Heart attack - Damage to heart muscle
  • Stroke - Brain damage from blocked or burst blood vessels
  • Kidney disease - Damage to kidneys affecting urine production
  • Vision loss - Damage to blood vessels in the eyes
  • Heart failure - Heart becomes too weak to pump blood

Blood Pressure Categories:

Category Systolic (Top Number) Diastolic (Bottom Number)
Normal Less than 120 Less than 80
Elevated 120-129 Less than 80
High Blood Pressure Stage 1 130-139 80-89
High Blood Pressure Stage 2 140 or higher 90 or higher
Hypertensive Crisis Higher than 180 Higher than 120

⚠️ Emergency: If your blood pressure is higher than 180/120, seek immediate medical help.

General Health Advice

When to Visit a Health Center

  • You have a fever that lasts more than 3 days
  • You have chest pain or difficulty breathing
  • You are confused or having difficulty thinking clearly
  • You are severely dehydrated or cannot keep fluids down
  • You have symptoms that are getting worse despite home care
  • You want to get a health screening or check-up

Keep These Records

  • Your blood pressure readings
  • Your blood sugar levels (if diabetic)
  • Medications you take and their doses
  • Allergies to medications
  • Date of last vaccinations
  • Results of any medical tests

Prevention is Better Than Cure

  • Maintain good hygiene habits
  • Stay physically active daily
  • Eat a balanced, nutritious diet
  • Drink plenty of clean water
  • Get regular health check-ups
  • Keep your home clean and free from mosquitoes
  • Use safe sanitation practices
